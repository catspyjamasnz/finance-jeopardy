# Show Me the Money — Scenario Review

For each team: the scenario, the three guided questions (Step 1), and the final recommendation prompt (Step 2). The "Principle" line is the intended takeaway (not shown to teams) — useful for checking the questions actually lead there.

---

## Scenario 1 — The Mid-Year Over-Enrolment

**Scenario:**
Your program launched in February with a budgeted intake of 120 students. By the March census you have 165 enrolled — a 38% over-enrolment. Great news for revenue, but your teaching team is already at capacity, student satisfaction scores are dipping, and two facilitators have flagged burnout. You have unspent budget in non-essential project work (a planned process-improvement initiative that hasn't started) but no line for additional teaching staff.

**Step 1 — Three guided questions:**
1. What does the extra revenue actually cost you to deliver well?
2. Is there money already in your budget that could be redirected to teaching support?
3. What happens to satisfaction scores and word-of-mouth if quality slips this term?

**Step 2 — Recommendation prompt:**
Now frame your recommendation. What would you tell the Finance team to do, and why?

**Principle (intended takeaway):**
Revenue you can't deliver on well isn't a win — and a budget line is a plan, not a rule.

## Scenario 2 — The Forecast That Says "Cut"

**Scenario:**
Mid-year, Finance shares a forecast showing your portfolio will land 15% under revenue — a couple of external corporate opportunities slipped and a program under-filled. You're asked to find savings to partially close the gap. Your costs are roughly: 55% people (including sessional facilitators), 20% platform and IT, 15% marketing, 10% other. Cutting facilitators hits delivery quality; cutting marketing risks the back-half pipeline; the platform costs are committed.

**Step 1 — Three guided questions:**
1. Which of your costs are committed versus discretionary right now?
2. What happens next year if you cut marketing to fix this year's gap?
3. Is a 'saving' really gone for good, or just pushed into next year?

**Step 2 — Recommendation prompt:**
Now frame your recommendation. What would you tell the Finance team to do, and why?

**Principle (intended takeaway):**
Not all costs are equally cuttable, and the cheapest cut to make is often the most expensive one to have made.

## Scenario 3 — Build vs Buy on a New Program

**Scenario:**
There's demand for a new professional program in an emerging field. You can build it in-house (your people, slower, cheaper cash cost, but it ties up capacity you'd otherwise use on existing delivery) or licence content and bring in expert contractors (faster to market, higher cash cost, less IP retained). The window of demand may not stay open long, and a competitor is rumoured to be moving.

**Step 1 — Three guided questions:**
1. How long is this demand window really likely to stay open?
2. What's the cost of being early but expensive, versus late but cheap?
3. Could a blend of build and buy work — and what would that look like in practice?

**Step 2 — Recommendation prompt:**
Now frame your recommendation. What would you tell the Finance team to do, and why?

**Principle (intended takeaway):**
The cheapest option on paper isn't the best if it loses you the market — weigh cost against speed, IP, and opportunity cost together.

## Scenario 4 — The Loss-Leader Program (Lifetime Value)

**Scenario:**
A new product runs at a slim margin — on its own numbers it barely washes its face. But it's where a large share of learners first encounter RMIT UP, and many go on to enrol in higher-value programs afterward. There's pressure to either lift its price (risking the volume that feeds everything else) or cut it to tidy up the portfolio. Either move looks sensible if you only read this one program's P&L.

**Step 1 — Three guided questions:**
1. What would you need to measure to know if this program is paying off elsewhere?
2. What happens to other programs if this one disappears?
3. Could repricing cost more in lost volume than it gains per learner?

**Step 2 — Recommendation prompt:**
Now frame your recommendation. What would you tell the Finance team to do, and why?

**Principle (intended takeaway):**
Some programs earn their keep through the lifetime value of the learners they bring in, not the margin on the program itself.

## Scenario 5 — The Year-End Surplus

**Scenario:**
Strong demand means your area will finish the year with a $150k surplus. Mitun is pushing you to spend it now on a delivery-platform upgrade that's on the wish-list but not urgent. Tony A wants it for capability training (and lots more team lunches) that would lift quality next year. Josh wants to take it back into improving the result. It's late October and whatever spend you don't commit to by Nov 30 is no longer available.

**Step 1 — Three guided questions:**
1. What would this decision look like if the deadline didn't exist?
2. Which option has the clearest link to next year's delivery quality?
3. Is handing the surplus back actually a loss, or just a clean start?

**Step 2 — Recommendation prompt:**
Now frame your recommendation. What would you tell the Finance team to do, and why?

**Principle (intended takeaway):**
Good stewardship looks the same in May as in October — you spend because the investment is right, not because the year is closing.
