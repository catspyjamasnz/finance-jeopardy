What is a budget?                                                 The budget is the target set at planning time (generally started in July and agreed by Board in September). What actually gets spent is different --- that\'s called actuals. The gap between them is called a variance.

A forecast is your best current view of what you\'ll spend or earn. Unlike a budget, it moves. Finance does this monthly. Good businesses update their forecast when things change --- not at year end.

  **\$200**    Day-to-day running costs - staff, software licences, travel, events, office supplies. What is OpEx? (Operating Expenditure)                                                 OpEx hits your budget immediately and directly. Most RMIT UP managers only deal with OpEx. CapEx (Capital Expenditure) is long-term asset spending and usually goes through a separate approval process.
  **\$400**    When RMIT UP runs an FS program, some costs --- like a teacher\'s salary or a room booking --- can be directly linked to that specific program. But other costs, like the finance team, HR, IT systems and building maintenance, are shared across the whole organisation. Finance distributes a share of these shared costs to each program using a formula. These shared costs, once allocated, are called?   What are overhead allocations (or shared service costs / central cost allocations)?   Overhead allocations often surprise managers --- a program can look profitable on direct costs alone but run at a deficit once overheads are added. Understanding that your program carries a share of the whole organisation\'s support costs is essential for reading your numbers accurately.
  **\$600**    An RMIT UP English language program charges students \$5,450 per course. The direct cost to deliver that course --- teacher salaries, materials, classroom costs --- is \$2,000 per student. The \$3,450 difference, expressed as a percentage of the \$5,450 fee, is this financial metric --- and it tells us how much is left to cover overhead before we make a profit.                                     What is gross margin? (Or: gross profit margin --- 63.3% in this example)             Gross margin = (Revenue minus Direct Costs) divided by Revenue. A higher gross margin means more money left over to cover shared costs like administration, IT and facilities. When enrolment numbers drop, gross margin compresses quickly because direct costs don\'t fall at the same rate as revenue.
