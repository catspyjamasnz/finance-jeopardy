**Show Me the Money**

Scenario-Based Learning --- Facilitator Pack

*RMIT UP --- We Lead program for non-finance people managers*

How to run
----------

Assign one scenario per team (or give every team the same one to compare
reasoning). Teams discuss for 10 minutes, then present their position to
the room. There is rarely a single right answer --- the value is in the
trade-offs and the reasoning. Suggested answers below are the "finance
lens": what a CFO would want managers to land on, plus the common traps.
Use them to steer the debrief, not as a marking key.

Scenario 1 --- The Mid-Year Over-Enrolment
==========================================

Your program launched in February with a budgeted intake of 120
students. By the March census you have 165 enrolled --- a 38%
over-enrolment. Great news for revenue, but your teaching team is
already at capacity, student satisfaction scores are dipping, and two
facilitators have flagged burnout. You have unspent budget in
non-essential project work (a planned process-improvement initiative
that hasn't started) but no line for additional teaching staff.

***Discuss and present back:** Do you spend to protect quality now, bank
the upside, or something in between? What do you ask Finance to flex,
and what's the risk of getting this wrong in either direction?*

**Suggested answer**

-   The extra revenue isn't free --- it carries a delivery cost and a
    reputation risk, so banking all of it is the wrong instinct.

-   Reinvest part of the upside into protecting quality (additional
    facilitator capacity, casual/sessional support).

-   Ask Finance to reallocate the unspent project budget to teaching
    resource --- money already in your envelope, just committed to
    lower-priority work that can wait.

-   Trap one: take the revenue and let quality slide, costing you
    retention, satisfaction scores, and the word-of-mouth that drives
    future intakes.

-   Trap two: over-spend chasing perfection on a cohort that's already
    enrolled.

-   Principle: revenue you can't deliver on well isn't a win --- and a
    budget line is a plan, not a rule.

Scenario 2 --- The Forecast That Says "Cut"
===========================================

Mid-year, Finance shares a forecast showing your portfolio will land 15%
under revenue --- a couple of external corporate opportunities slipped
and an program under-filled. You're asked to find savings to partially
close the gap. Your costs are roughly: 55% people (including sessional
facilitators), 20% platform and IT, 15% marketing, 10% other. Cutting
facilitators hits delivery quality; cutting marketing risks in the
back-half pipeline; the platform costs are committed.

***Discuss and present back:** Where do you find the savings, and what
do you protect at all costs? Which "saving" is actually just deferring a
cost into next year?*

**Suggested answer**

-   First, split your costs into committed and discretionary --- you can
    only realistically act on the discretionary half this year.

-   Platform/IT (20%) is largely committed contracts, so treat it as off
    the table for in-year savings regardless of how big the line looks.

-   Sessional facilitator cost should fall by itself as cohorts shrink
    --- that's cost matching activity, not a saving you have to "find."

-   Marketing (15%) is the easiest to switch off and the most dangerous
    to touch --- cutting it now to fix this year starves the pipeline
    that fills next year's cohorts.

-   So look first to discretionary, deferrable spend: non-essential
    projects, travel, holding a vacancy open rather than backfilling.

-   When you present to Finance, label each saving as either genuine
    (gone for good) or a deferral (a cost simply pushed into next year)
    --- don't let a deferral be counted as a real win.

-   Principle: not all costs are equally cuttable, and the cheapest cut
    to make is often the most expensive one to have made.

Scenario 3 --- Build vs Buy on a New Program
============================================

There's demand for a new professional program in an emerging field. You
can build it in-house (your people, slower, cheaper cash cost, but it
ties up capacity you'd otherwise use on existing delivery) or licence
content and bring in expert contractors (faster to market, higher cash
cost, less IP retained). The window of demand may not stay open long,
and a competitor is rumoured to be moving.

***Discuss and present back:** Build, buy, or blend? How do you weigh
speed-to-market against cost and IP, and what would tip the decision?*

**Suggested answer**

-   The real trade-off is speed and certainty (buy) versus cost and IP
    retention (build) --- name which one matters most for this specific
    opportunity.

-   If the demand window is genuinely short and a competitor is moving,
    time-to-market may be worth the higher cash cost --- being late to a
    closing window can mean zero return regardless of how cheaply you
    built it.

-   A blend often wins: licence/contract to launch fast, then bring
    delivery and IP in-house once the program is proven.

-   Factor in opportunity cost --- building in-house isn't "free," it
    consumes capacity you could use on existing revenue-generating
    delivery.

-   Trap: choosing "build" purely because the cash cost looks lower,
    while ignoring the IP you'd lose by buying and the revenue you'd
    miss by being slow.

-   Principle: the cheapest option on paper isn't the best if it loses
    you the market --- weigh cost against speed, IP, and opportunity
    cost together.

Scenario 4 --- The Loss-Leader Program (Lifetime Value)
=======================================================

A new product runs at a slim margin --- on its own numbers it barely
washes its face. But it's where a large share of learners first
encounter RMIT UP, and many go on to enrol in higher-value programs
afterward. There's pressure to either lift its price (risking the volume
that feeds everything else) or cut it to tidy up the portfolio. Either
move looks sensible if you only read this one program's P&L.

***Discuss and present back:** Do you keep it, reprice it, or cut it?
How does learner lifetime value change the picture, and what would you
need to measure to be confident?*

**Suggested answer**

-   Judge it on learner lifetime value, not the standalone program
    margin --- its job is acquisition, and the return shows up in the
    higher-value programs learners take next.

-   Measure the conversion: what proportion of these learners go on to
    enrol elsewhere with RMIT UP, and what's the average downstream
    revenue per learner?

-   If that downstream value is strong, the program is a profitable
    acquisition channel even at a slim direct margin --- cutting it
    would quietly shrink the pipeline into everything else.

-   Repricing is the riskiest lever: a higher price that reduces entry
    volume can cost far more in lost lifetime value than it gains per
    head.

-   Trap: "tidying up" a low-margin program and only discovering months
    later that downstream enrolments fell with it.

-   Principle: some programs earn their keep through the lifetime value
    of the learners they bring in, not the margin on the program itself.

Scenario 5 --- The Year-End Surplus
===================================

Strong demand means your area will finish the year with a \$150k
surplus. Mitun is pushing you to spend it now on a delivery-platform
upgrade that's on the wish-list but not urgent. Tony A wants it for
capability training (and lots more team lunches) that would lift quality
next year. Josh wants to take it back into improving the result. It's
late October and whatever spend you don't commit to by Nov 30 is no
longer available.

***Discuss and present back:** Do you spend it, and on what --- or hand
it back? How do you make sure the decision reads as good stewardship
rather than a year-end spend-up?*

**Suggested answer**

-   Decide on merit, not the calendar --- the deadline creates pressure
    to spend, but "we'll lose it otherwise" is not a business case.

-   Of the options, ask which actually strengthens next year: the
    capability training has a clearer link to delivery quality than a
    "nice to have" platform upgrade that isn't urgent.

-   If neither is genuinely ready and worthwhile, handing it back is the
    disciplined answer --- starting next year flat is not a loss.

-   Trap: committing to the platform upgrade just because it's on the
    wish-list and the money's about to disappear.

-   Principle: good stewardship looks the same in May as in October ---
    you spend because the investment is right, not because the year is
    closing.
